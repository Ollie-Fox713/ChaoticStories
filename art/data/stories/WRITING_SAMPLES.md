# Writing Samples

These sample passages are tone references for story writers working in `assets/data/stories/{locale}/`.

They are not schema examples. They are voice examples.

The current house style across the existing Perim stories tends to be:

- second-person
- concrete physical detail
- local stakes before epic stakes
- understated humor instead of overt jokes
- tribal and regional specificity
- wonder grounded in labor, place, or culture rather than abstraction

Use these as reference points when writing new daily stories, chain entries, or branching scenes.

When building a 5-story batch for a single location, at least 1 story should be an explicit wonder / immersion story. That story should slow down enough to let the player really take in the place.

## Funny / Levity

The embassy annex in Kiru City was designed by someone who believed paperwork could civilize anything. Broad stone counters, neat queue ropes, labeled windows, polished floors. Then the actual citizens arrived. Today's issue is a Mipedian trade representative who insists that becoming invisible should not count as leaving the line because, in his words, "my position remained conceptually stable even if my visibility did not." The OverWorld clerk handling the dispute has the distant expression of someone who has already lost an argument with reality this morning and sees no reason not to lose another.

She slides a wax tablet toward you without looking up. "Please mark whether the missing applicant is invisible, gliding overhead, concealed under ceremonial fabric, or being difficult on purpose." Somewhere to your left, an unseen voice begins objecting to the phrasing. The clerk dips her stylus, sighs, and lowers her voice. "For the record, the fourth category is the one we use most."

## Horror / Spooky

Night duty at the Doors of the Deepmines teaches the same lesson every time: silence is not empty. The chamber is cold, the bronze slabs are older than anyone can date, and every sound seems to arrive from farther away than it should. Your shift partner has been stationed here long enough to stop pretending calm makes him brave. He keeps a notebook, a spear, and a flask he touches more often than he drinks from. "Most nights," he says, "nothing happens. That's what makes it hard. Your mind starts helping."

The sound comes in the third hour. Not a knock. Not a scrape. Something slower than either, like a hand laid flat against metal and drawn downward with patient pressure from the other side. Bronze should carry it cleanly, but this reaches you warped and stretched, as if the door is translating it badly. Your partner goes still. Even the torchfire seems to shrink. He does not step closer. He does not call out. After a long moment he says, quietly enough that the bronze might not hear, "Don't answer it. We don't know if it likes being answered."

## Amazement / Wonder

At the edge of Cordac Falls, the mist never really settles. It drifts through the lower terraces of Kiru City in a fine, constant veil, beading on railings, soaking stone, feeding moss in cracks too small to notice until the light hits them. At this hour the sun is still low enough that the falls are more brightness than water, a white column dropping out of the sky and breaking into wind before it reaches the basin. The bridge crystals strung along the span are waking one by one, drinking in that scattered light and answering with a softer glow of their own, pale gold at first, then blue, then a clear silver that seems less illuminated than alive.

Workers crossing the bridge barely look up. A courier balances a stack of sealed scroll tubes against one shoulder. Two Muges argue quietly over resonance intervals without breaking stride. A child leans over the railing and points into the mist where shapes of water and light keep almost becoming something recognizable before dissolving again. That is the part that catches you: not that the place is beautiful, though it is, but that everyone here has made a life inside beauty so routine they can trust it. The falls thunder, the crystals answer, the city holds itself above open air, and for one long moment it all seems impossibly fragile until you realize the opposite is true. This is what endurance looks like in Perim when it has had time to become graceful.

## Amazement / Wonder, Danian

Deep in Mount Pillar, there are garden caverns where the hive's fungus beds stretch farther than torchlight would carry if torches were needed there at all. They are not needed. The fungus provides its own light, soft blue at the edges, richer toward the center of each bed, pulsing so faintly and so slowly that at first you think the motion belongs to your eyes adjusting. Then you stand still long enough to understand that the glow is real, a living rhythm passing through the cavern floor in patient waves. Moisture clings to the stone overhead, catching that blue light and returning it as dim stars across the ceiling.

The workers move carefully between the beds with harvesting baskets and moisture probes, their shadows long and strange in the bioluminescent glow. No one raises their voice. Even routine instructions come out quieter here, as if the cavern itself encourages reverence without ever asking for it. A nursery tender pauses beside one of the brighter clusters and lays a hand just above the warm surface, not touching, only feeling the heat of growth. "When people talk about the hive," she says, "they usually mean the soldiers, or the queens, or the wars." Her gaze stays on the glowing beds. "But this is what keeps all of that alive." Looking across the blue-lit terraces, with the stone ceiling glittering and the fungus breathing light into the dark, it is easy to believe you are standing inside the body's gentlest organ.

## Serious

In Kiru City, the older soldiers do not begin with the names of the dead. They begin with the things that failed them: patrol routes that looked secure until they weren't, scouts who reported exactly what they saw and still missed what mattered, commanders forced to choose between two bad answers and then live long enough to see the third one they should have found. They speak in logistics first because logistics are survivable. You can put supply failures on a chart. You can redraw a perimeter. You can train around a mistake.

Only after that does someone say Raznus. The conversation narrows. Shoulders settle. No one reaches for rhetoric because rhetoric would be disrespectful to what actually happened. One veteran rubs a thumb across an old scar and says, "People think grief comes after the fighting. Sometimes it walks onto the field wearing a familiar face." No one answers him. They don't need to. In that silence, the whole city feels older.

## Mystery

The scanner spike is clean enough to be convincing and wrong enough to be impossible. It appears under the ridge, inside the ridge, and for one brief pulse thirty feet above the ridge where the air should be empty except for heat shimmer. You recalibrate twice, shielding the screen from the sun, checking the lens, checking the coil, checking your own hands for tremor. The readings repeat with almost insulting precision. Whatever this is, it is either malfunctioning in a very disciplined way or telling you something the rest of the world has not agreed to admit yet.

The scout beside you kneels and presses two fingers to the stone like she expects it to answer her directly. "Smugglers use this pass," she says. "Muges hide caches in the upper clefts. Mipedians bury markers where only Mipedians can read them. None of that looks like this." She straightens slowly, still watching the ridge instead of the scanner. "Hidden things in Perim usually want to remain hidden. This feels different." The next pulse hits before she finishes the thought, and this time the stone beneath your boots hums with it.

## Danian / Workaday Unease

The fungus gardens beneath Mount Pillar are warmer than the tunnels around them, full of damp blue light and the smell of living compost. Workers move between the beds with moisture probes, clay scoops, and marking sticks while the overseers watch for changes in glow pattern the way other people watch weather. A healthy bed shines evenly. A thirsty one dulls at the edges. Contamination follows drainage lines. The hive knows these rules because the hive has lived by them for generations.

That is why the dark patch in bed seven is wrong in a way everyone notices immediately. It isn't ragged or spreading. It is a near-perfect circle, dead at the center and healthy around the edge, as if something chose a point and worked outward with care. The overseer crouches beside it, pushes her probe into the substrate, then withdraws it without reading the scale. She is no longer thinking about irrigation. "Natural problems leak," she says. "They creep. They leave a trail." She taps the edge of the circle once with the side of the probe. "This has intent."

## UnderWorld / Threat Presence

Messages move quickly in UnderWorld City, but the city can tell the difference between speed and danger. Speed shouts. Danger clears a path. The courier who enters Chaor's hall does not bark for passage or throw his name around to make lesser creatures scramble. He simply keeps moving, helmet scorched, one shoulder blackened with soot, carrying an iron seal in both hands as if it might burn through him if he grips it wrong. By the time he reaches the foot of the dais, everyone in the chamber has stopped pretending not to watch.

Chaor takes the seal, glances at the mark stamped into the metal, and the room changes shape without a single stone moving. No roar. No display. No easy anger for the crowd to understand. Just stillness, hard and immediate, the kind that makes every creature present recalculate what kind of day this has become. A warlord in fury is familiar. A warlord thinking is worse. One of the guards near the pillar lowers his eyes. Another takes a half step back before catching himself. Whatever message can make Chaor go quiet has already spread beyond the seal. It is now in the air, and everyone is breathing it.

## OverWorld / Quiet Reverence

The Peace Garden on the edge of Kiru City's influence does not impress the way the training courts or towers do. It has no grand approach, no ceremonial gate, no choir of Mugic resonances announcing that you have arrived somewhere important. It is simply a tended space of old roots, low water channels, flowering vines, and a silence so deliberate it feels cultivated. The gardener tells you the plants are sensitive to intent, but the word "sensitive" undersells the experience. They do not merely react. They evaluate.

When you kneel to water the sunward bed, the nearest stems lean a fraction toward you, as cautious as wild creatures deciding whether you are worth the risk. The movement is small enough to deny if you need to, but impossible to miss once you have seen it. Halfway down the row, a flower opens that had been closed when you began, unfolding petals in a color that doesn't sit comfortably with any name you know. The gardener notices your expression and speaks more softly than before. "That one doesn't bloom for everyone," she says. It is not praise. It sounds more like responsibility.
